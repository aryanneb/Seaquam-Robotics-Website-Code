What You Need


To get started with your design notebook, it is evident that you actually have a notebook. We at Seaquam Robotics recommend the original VEX Robotics Design book, as it is generally all most teams will require. It only costs CA$14.99, making it a fairly priced notebook for the quality you get. The pages consist of dotted lines and spaces for dating, witnesses and even title headings.

It is important to note that this notebook is not required. You can buy cheaper or more expensive notebooks, whichever is your preference. As a general rule, try to buy notebooks that have dotted patterns instead of lines, as it makes the drawing of accurate diagrams much easier. For those looking for the ultimate note-booking experience, Moleskine notebooks provide excellent paper.

As for the tool you use to write on the paper, Seaquam Robotics uses pens for the main bodies of text and pencils for complex diagrams or drawings. This is because patents require you to use pen so that they can verify the legitimacy of your drawings. This is a good habit to get used to in the future, as most design notebooks would be written in pen.

Table of Contents


The Table of Contents is one of the most important parts about any Design Notebook. It dictates to the reader the overall flow of your writing and allows them to quickly see the progress and organization of your thought processes.

How does one create a good Table of Contents?

There are three main components that go into creating a good table of contents entry: Page Number, Title and Date. In particular, creating a concise title can be difficult for most teams. For example, if my team had been working on our chassis that day, I would personally title that entry as "Chassis Development." This short title allows the reader to easily understand the message and meaning behind the entry entry without reading it. The other two components just make the document easier to find and verify.

Another general design choice with the notebook is having an entry be only about two-three pages at maximum. Any entries longer than this should be separated into different entries instead, as the content being discussed is too much for one heading.

Everyday Reflections


Building on the idea of adding entries that are short and concise is the general practice of making entries everyday. This makes the design process more in-depth and thoughtfully considered. It also allows you to be more reflective about your own thought process after a long work session. In this case, 'everyday reflections' refer to creating a reflection after each session you have where you work on your robot.

At Seaquam, teams that are the most competitive make these reflections after each Robotics session, analyzing their choices and making goals for the future simultaneously.

There are many methods which you can implement into your everyday Robotics work session to make this habit stick. Some teams may incentivize the notebook, offering someone write in it instead of having to clean up for their team after the session. At Seaquam, many students are given the 'designer' role so that they can fulfill the duties of creating the design notebook.

Get creative with how you want your team to write daily in the notebook!

Images, Drawings and CAD


Showing what you are trying to build by creating visuals such as Images, Drawings, or Computer-Aided-Design (CAD), make it much easier for the person reading your notebook to better understand the topic you are elaborating on.

Judges love additions of these visuals. For good reason, too! They make it much easier to see the thought process of the team.

"I'm not good at drawing, does that mean I can't make the design book?"

Not at all! Practice makes perfect, so the more designs you draw the better you will eventually get at it. If you want to forgo the drawings completely, CAD makes it much easier for even the worst artists to make realistic models. You can then print out screenshots of the CAD in multiple different angles, or even show the CAD to the judges itself.

"How should I insert these visuals into my notebook"

You can either draw them out by hand, or print them out then paste them in with glue or tape. Either of those methods is functional and will not dock you any points, making it the most time-effective solution.

The Processes


There are honestly a ton of different design processes that explain how we create solutions for problems in engineering and robotics.

Most students are familiar with some variation of this one:

Define the Problem → Collect Information → Brainstorm and Analyze → Develop Solutions/Build a Model → Present your Ideas to Others for Feedback → Improve your Design and repeat the process.

One more straightforward examples is the NASA Engineering Design Process.

Ask → Imagine → Plan → Develop Create → Experiment → Improve and Repeat

For any good design book, it is important you find a design process which you find easiest to connect to your teams build habits and and try using that one in your Notebook.

After finding which one you like, make sure you are consistent with this process in your notebook. Each design choice you make should be explicitly connected to the process. This improves your knowledge of the processes and shows the Judges you understand the actions you are taking.

Uses


If you have made anything, chances are you've subconsciously used a design process. Engineers find themselves constantly using these design processes, as they are creating new objects, structures, products even that require elegant and creative designs. The best way to improve your ability to create more efficient solutions to the problems at hand.

The documentation of this process also for more in-depth critical thinking, as sometimes simply thinking about the problems can lead ot oversight or even inability to recognize future problems. The documentation process also makes it much easier to construct whatever it is that you are creating again.

Those who are better at documenting this process can make their reflections and observations so clear and descriptive that anyone could replicate their creations, a goal that any Robotics students should strive to achieve.

Goal-Setting


One universal aspect of designing and planning is setting goals. When creating goals, it is important to set SMART Goals.

S-Specific
When creating goals, make sure that they are specific to the task at hand. They should not try to extend in too many directions, as that can be overwhelming and difficult to narrowly focus on.

M-Measurable
It is important to always have goals that are measurable. These can make it much easier to determine when you have actually completed the task you set out to complete. It also allows you to track your progress more clearly, giving more purpose in your venture.

A-Achievable
Making goals that are achievable in Robotics is a must. As a first-year team, your capabilities to compete are generally lower than those of another team who has been in the programming for much longer. Even with senior teams, most graduating students spend less time at Robotics simply because they have so many other responsibilities in their senior year.

R-Relevant
Relevant goals drive your team. They provide the basis for questions such as 'Does this seem worthwhile?' These goals have meaning behind them, instead of simply being infused with numbers and calculations. Make your goals personal and you will be more likely to work harder for them.

T-Timely
The hardest part about maintaining a goal is meeting the time constraints. This can be especially difficult for newer teams, as they don't have much experience working at Robotics, leading to a distortion of time. With experience, you should learn how to set more timely goals. Also, if you don't meet a deadline, don't worry! Just try to catch up!

Documentation


Now that you have learned a little about the Design Process and Goal-Setting, you are ready to start working on your Design Notebook.

First and foremost, it is important that you describe these processes in your OWN words. That means to paraphrase and interpret the design processes in your own words. This will allow you show off your design knowledge and will impress any judge. Ideally, you would also go through each stage and recognize what this part of the process means to you.

Now that you have cited your design processes, it is time to start creating journal entries. When creating these, linking back to your design method is extremely important. Spend a few lines at the end asking where your team is in their design stages and determine what the next step will be.

Simply by following these steps of documentation, you should be scoring much more effectively in the design areas with your design book.

The Full Rubric


Here is the full marking rubric for your design book:

Process Marks


As you can see in the rubric, a majority of your points come from the Engineering design process marks. That is why this resources section has had such a strong emphasis on the process behind the notebook.



Out of the possible 45 points given in this rubric, a majority (30) of these marks are pertaining to the design process used by the team. That is why it is important to have a strong focus on the actual process of your notebook.

Why use a Design Notebook?


As is evident in the rubric, using anything other than a bound notebook significantly reduces your chances of scoring highly. Out of the possible 45 points awarded, you limit yourself to 40, which effectively makes your notebooks maximum score 88.9% of what it would have been previously.



The main explanation for why VEX decided to include this is so that they can verify that you are simply not removing pages and putting them in different orders, as is possible with a different notebook construction. Teams who do this can easily fudge their data entries, or change their reflection dates. Engineers often use notebooks like the ones we use in VEX, so it is reasonable for VEX to include this rule.

The Other Marks


If you have been following the design process and making your notebook the way an engineer would, your notebook should also consequently score highly in these last two categories.



The usefulness and repeatability should come from the development of ideas in your design process and the record of team management can easily be scored highly on as long as your team updates their design notebook often. We have given some tips and advice on how to do these above, so read through some of the sections to get a better idea on how to improve on these categories.

How Many Notebooks Should We Use?


Use as many as you want!

If you require more space than one notebook just use another! Some teams have also opted to create specific design notebooks for aspects of their robot that are more complicated and developed than other parts. For example, teams in the past have used one notebook for the design of their robot and the kept track of their programming inside of a completely different notebook.

What if my team doesn't fill up our first notebook?

Don't worry! Just because some teams have more pages does not necessarily mean that those pages are of high quality. Don't worry about the amount as long as you are actually creating quality content.

Judges Presentations


Judges presentations are when you highlight your design process to the judges! This gives you an opportunity to show your expertise and knowledge in designing, as well as getting constructive criticism in the development of your ideas.

What do I need for these presentations

Your robot, your team and your notebook are the bare necessities. Many teams also like to create slideshow presentations for the judges, as it keeps their ideas organized and allows them to show off their notebook and robot well. We recommend everyone to give a presentation, even if you don't feel like you have anything to present. This opportunity gives you a chance to simply talk about your robot, as well as answer questions on how you might have built it.

Awards


If your team has an excellent design notebook, you are in contention for many awards.

Design Award
Evidently, submitting a design notebook will allow you to compete for the Design Award. This award is given to the team with the most clear and effective application of the design process in their meeting with the judges as well as their Engineering Notebook.

Think Award
This award is generally given to the team with the most sophisticated, or most innovative use of programming in their robot. By having a clear focus on programming in your notebook, you increase your chances of winning this award.

Amaze and Judges Awards
This award is given to any team that generally impresses the judges. One important criteria for winning this award is having a good design notebook that impresses the judges.

Build Award
The build award is given to the team with the most structurally sound building of their robot. To show the design process behind why you would have built your robot in the particular way you did improves your chances of winning this award.

Create Award
This award is given to the team who is most creative. This can be with their implementation of certain unique design choices. Reasons for why this would be beneficial can be explained and effectively explored in the notebook.

Excellence Award
The Holy Grail of awards, Excellence. This award is given to the team with an overall focus on excellence. Not only does your notebook have to be immaculately constructed to be in contention for this award, but the other aspects of your robot must match this description too.
